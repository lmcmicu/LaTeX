\subsubsection*{Introduction}
In the preface \citep{vonwright1969} to the \emph{Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus},\footnote{\citealt*[200]{vonmises1957}} Wittgenstein writes:

\begin{quote}

... the \emph{truth} of the thoughts that are here communicated seems to me unassailable and definitive. I therefore believe myself to have found, on all essential points, the final solution of the problems.\footnote{\citealt*[201]{vonmises1957}}

\end{quote}

For new readers \citep{Salmon1989}, the idea is that if we are to take fully seriously Wittgenstein's comments in (6.54), then we must conclude that Wittgenstein does not intend to put forth any philosophical doctrines. Diamond writes, for example:

\begin{quotation}

\noindent Are we going to keep the idea that there is something or other in reality that we gesture at, however badly, when we speak of the `logical form of reality', so that \emph{it, what} we were gesturing at, is there but cannot be expressed in words?

\emph{That} is what I call chickening out. What counts as not chickening out is then this, roughly: to throw away the ladder is, among other things, to throw away the attempt to take seriously the language of `features of reality'.\footnote{\citealt*[202]{vonmises1957}}

\end{quotation}
